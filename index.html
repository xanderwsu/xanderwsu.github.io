Ubiquitous throughout Pompeii‚Äôs streets were public benches. When it came to eating and drinking, the average Pompeiian left their home and ate out, for the limited and dense living arrangements didn‚Äôt have space for a pantry or kitchen, and any kind of cooking could be a disastrous fire waiting to happen.

Politics appears to have dominated the Pompeiian social scene. Every year, four magistrates were elected: two duumvir and two aedile, with the latter being more of a junior position to the former. Together they would fill and oversee the town council, a body that would operate the civic management of Pompeii, such as the building of public spaces or managing the treasury. Being a councillor granted one certain privileges, such as the best seating at public events. However, the role of duumvir trumped them all, which is likely why archaeologists have unearthed over 2,500 campaign posters throughout the city of Pompeii, a town of roughly 12,000 people.

Most of these posters are rather plainly stated slogans: ‚ÄúI beg you to elect Publius Furius duumvir, a good man.‚Äù Sometimes a little more, but not much, will be added, such as ‚ÄúHe brings good bread,‚Äù or ‚Äúgreat giver of games.‚Äù We even have examples of negative or satirical ads: ‚ÄúThe little thieves ask for Vatia as aedile‚Äù and ‚ÄúThe dice-throwers ask for Cn. To bathe is to be Roman and like the rest of the empire, Pompeii featured a multitude of commercial and private baths, although most were in a state of disrepair ever since the great earthquake of 62 CE. A bathing complex would be a place to socialize and relax while enjoying a steam, a scrub down, a massage, some sunbathing, and even games played with a ball. They were the ultimate source of Roman R&R.

Baths are also considered a great social equalizer, as everyone visited the baths, from the rich to the poor, the elite and the enslaved, even emperors would visit public baths.

Despite the immense importance of bathing in Roman society, the bathwater itself was surprisingly a disgusting cesspool by modern standards. It was not chemically treated like our modern pools and the water was only infrequently changed, thus leaving behind all the dirt, grim, and bacteria of all the people who entered it. Even Roman medical professionals would advise to not enter a bath with an open wound lest they wish to contract an infection and gangrene.

Although your average Pompeiian lacked the space to grow cattle for food, there is evidence that some still managed to raise livestock for consumption. Found in Pompeii are glirariums, or dormouse jars, which were terracotta jars that housed mouse-like rodents for the intended purpose of fattening them up for consumption. The jars were poked with air holes and fitted with a lid to keep the dormouse in darkness, perhaps to induce them into sleeping and growing fat as opposed to muscle. Inside were layered ledges for running in circles and food trays that could be filled from openings on the outside. Multiple recipes have been discovered in Roman cookbooks, including stuffing the dormice with pork, pepper, nuts, fennel, and fish sauce or simply dipping them in honey and topping with sesame seeds.
